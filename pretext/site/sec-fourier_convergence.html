Section 5.5 Convergence of Fourier series

Consider the Fourier series for \(f(x)=x\) over the range \((-\pi, \pi)\) which is

\begin{align*} x \amp = 2 \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n} \sin(nx)\\ \amp = 2 \left ( \sin(x) - \frac{1}{2} \sin(2x) + \frac{1}{3} \sin(3x) - \frac{1}{4} \sin(4x) + \dots \right ). \end{align*}

Suppose we attempt to differentiate both sides of this equation, doing so term-by-term to the right hand side. This will produce the equation

\begin{equation*} 1 = 2 \big ( \cos(x) - \cos(2x) + \cos(3x) - \cos(4x) + \dots \big ). \end{equation*}

However if we evaluate both sides of this equation at \(x=0\) we get

\begin{equation*} 1 = 2 (1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + \dots ), \end{equation*}

which is plainly nonsense. The problem is that we have assumed we could differentiate the right hand side of the Fourier series term-by-term, i.e. we assumed that

\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx} \left ( \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n \sin(nx) \right ) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{d}{dx} \left ( b_n \sin(nx) \right ). \end{equation*}

Of course, the derivative of a finite sum is always equal to the sum of the derivatives, as differentiation is a linear operator. But infinite sums are a different matter. Term-by-term differentiation (or integration) of an infinite series of functions (e.g. Taylor series, Fourier series, ...) is only valid if the series converges uniformly. This is a stronger form of convergence than the usual pointwise convergence.

The two forms of convergence for sequences of functions are defined below. It is important to realise that uniform convergence is a far stronger condition on the bahaviour of the sequence of functions than pointwise convergence. So not all sequences of functions that converge in a pointwise fashion will converge uniformly.

Definition 5.5.1. Pointwise convergence.

A sequence of functions \(f_n:\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) converges pointwise to a limit function \(f:\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) if and only if for each argument \(a \in \mathbb{R}\) the sequence of values \(\left \{ f_n(a) \right \}_{n=1}^\infty\) converges to the limit value \(f(a)\text{,}\) i.e. for all \(a \in \mathbb{R}\) and for all \(\epsilon \gt 0\) there exists \(N \in \mathbb{N}\) such that \(|f_n(a) - f(a)| \lt \epsilon\text{.}\)

Both forms of convergence can also take place over restricted domains, i.e. not necessarily the whole of \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) This should be made clear wherever they appear.

Definition 5.5.2. Uniform convergence.

A sequence of functions \(f_n:\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) converges uniformly to a limit function \(f:\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) across the whole domain \(\mathbb{R}\) if and only if for each argument \(a \in \mathbb{R}\) the sequence of values \(\left \{ f_n(a) \right \}_{n=1}^\infty\) converges to the limit value \(f(a)\) and moreover there is an overall bound on the rate that this convergence takes place at. Put formally, this is, for all \(\epsilon \gt 0\) there exists \(N \in \mathbb{N}\) such that for all \(a \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) \(|f_n(a) - f(a)| \lt \epsilon\text{.}\)

Theorem 5.5.3.

The Fourier series of the function \(f\) over \((-\pi, \pi)\) is uniformly convergent if \(f\) is continuous on \((-\pi, \pi)\text{,}\) \(\frac{df}{dx}\) is piece-wise continuous on \((-\pi, \pi)\) and \(f\) is periodic of period \(2\pi\) so that \(f(-\pi)=f(\pi)\text{.}\)

Theorem 5.5.4. Parseval's theorem.

If \(f(x)\) has period \(2\pi\) and its Fourier series

\begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{1}{2} a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \cos(nx) + \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n \sin(nx), \end{equation*}

converges uniformly over \((-\pi,\pi)\) then

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^\pi \left ( f(x) \right )^2 \, dx = \frac{1}{4} a_0^2 + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{n=1}^\infty \left ( a_n^2 + b_n^2 \right ) . \end{equation*}

Parseval's theorem can be used to determine the sum of particular series.

Example 5.5.5.

The Fourier series for the half-way rectified sine wave

\begin{equation*} f(x) = \begin{cases} 0 \amp , -\pi \lt x \leq 0 \\ \sin(x) \amp, 0 \lt x \leq \pi \end{cases} , \end{equation*}

is the series

\begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi} + \frac{1}{2} \sin(x) -\frac{2}{\pi} \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{4n^2 -1} \cos(2n x). \end{equation*}

Use Parseval's theorem to find the sum of the series

\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{\left ( 4n^2 - 1 \right )^2}. \end{equation*}
