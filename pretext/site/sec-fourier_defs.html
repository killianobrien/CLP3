Section 1.1 Fourier's theorem and initial definitions

Theorem 1.1.1.

If \(f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) is a periodic function of period \(2 \pi\) and in the interval \((-\pi, \pi)\) has

  1. at most a finite number of discontinuities ,

  2. at most a finite number of maxima and minima,

then there exist a set of coefficients

\begin{equation*} a_0, a_1, a_2, \dots , b_1, b_2 , \dots \in \mathbb{R}, \end{equation*}

such that at any point of \(x\) of continuity of \(f\) we have

\begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{1}{2} a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \cos(nx) + \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n \sin(nx), \end{equation*}

and at points of discontinuity, \(x_d\text{,}\) the series satisfies

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2} \left ( f(x_d^+) + f(x_d^-) \right ) = \frac{1}{2} a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \cos(nx) + \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n \sin(nx), \end{equation*}

where

\begin{equation*} f(x_d^+) = \lim_{x \downarrow x_d} f(x), \quad f(x_d^-) = \lim_{x \uparrow x_d} f(x). \end{equation*}

The series of sines and cosines is referred to as the Fourier series for \(f\text{.}\) It agrees with \(f\) at all points of continuity of \(f\) and takes the average value of the left and right-handed limits of \(f\) at points of discontinuity.

The problem of finding a Fourier series for a given function \(f\) is the problem of determining the values of the Fourier coefficients \(a_0, a_n , b_n, \, \, (n \geq 1)\text{,}\) of \(f\text{.}\)

The existence of Fourier series, and the method for finding the coefficients rely on the fact that the family of functions

\begin{equation*} F= \left \{ 1, \cos(nx), \sin(nx) \, : \, n \geq 1 \right \}, \end{equation*}

form an orthogonal basis for the infinite-dimensional vector space of functions satisfying the conditions in Fourier's theorem with respect to the inner-product

\begin{equation*} \langle \phi, \psi \rangle = \int_{-\pi}^\pi \phi(x) \psi(x) \, dx . \end{equation*}

That is

\begin{equation*} \forall \phi, \psi \in F , \phi \neq \psi \Rightarrow \langle \phi, \psi \rangle = 0. \end{equation*}

The various cases of the orthogonality of \(F\) can be proved by evaluating the integrals using standard trigonometric substitutions.
