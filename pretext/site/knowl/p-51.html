Consider the Fourier series for \(f(x)=x\) over the range \((-\pi, \pi)\) which is

\begin{align*} x \amp = 2 \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n} \sin(nx)\\ \amp = 2 \left ( \sin(x) - \frac{1}{2} \sin(2x) + \frac{1}{3} \sin(3x) - \frac{1}{4} \sin(4x) + \dots \right ). \end{align*}

Suppose we attempt to differentiate both sides of this equation, doing so term-by-term to the right hand side. This will produce the equation

\begin{equation*} 1 = 2 \big ( \cos(x) - \cos(2x) + \cos(3x) - \cos(4x) + \dots \big ). \end{equation*}

However if we evaluate both sides of this equation at \(x=0\) we get

\begin{equation*} 1 = 2 (1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + \dots ), \end{equation*}

which is plainly nonsense. The problem is that we have assumed we could differentiate the right hand side of the Fourier series term-by-term, i.e. we assumed that

\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dx} \left ( \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n \sin(nx) \right ) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{d}{dx} \left ( b_n \sin(nx) \right ). \end{equation*}

Of course, the derivative of a finite sum is always equal to the sum of the derivatives, as differentiation is a linear operator. But infinite sums are a different matter. Term-by-term differentiation (or integration) of an infinite series of functions (e.g. Taylor series, Fourier series, \dots) is only valid if the series converges uniformly. This is a stronger form of convergence than the usual pointwise convergence.
